Indeed, perhaps the most striking thing about the retributive murder in this episode is that is not an isolated incident: retaliation for perceived wrongdoing is a recurrent, if not almost omnipresent, motif in how narrative violence against the religious other is emplotted in Vīramāheśvara literature. Wherever we find Jain basadis being demolished or heterodox heads impaled on stakes, more often than not, the Śaiva perpetrators perceived the victims to have been guilty of some prior crime. Recall the case of Ekanta Ramayya, whose self-beheading was precipitated by a Jain who happened to speak ill of Śiva.110 

Other cases abound, such as the devotee Hiriya Nacayya, who according to the Basavapurāṇamu assembled a group of thirteen Śaiva chiefs to launch a homicidal incursion into the Jain village of Māruḍiga. While the text has elided much of the previous chain of events, we are informed that Māruḍiga is a Jain majority town with only a single Śaiva temple, and that the Jain community, for reasons unknown, had previously murdered the Saiva priest of that temple.!!! This was the justification, according to the Basavapurāṇamu's emplotment, for the demolition of seven hundred basadis and the beheading of all Jina images in the village.
In other words, all of these cases not only attest to retributive violence but also frame it as a response to a perceived wrong inflicted upon Śiva himself, Śiva's devotees, or property under the ownership of Śaiva institutions— that is, sivadroha.112 

Even the most famously alleged Viraśaiva murder, as it turns out, the assassination of Bijjala of the Kalachuri dynasty of Kalyāna, is attributed by Somanatha to a suspicion of sivadroha on the part of the king himself.113 In this context, sivadroha is not the betrayal of one's personal allegiance, but the violation of the normative or legal prescriptions that governed the Śaiva domain.1114 

After all, in the case of the devotee's own persons, the terms "Śaiva" and "Mäheśvara” mean little more than the fact that the individual in question bears a direct, if not intrinsic, connection to Śiva himself, not unlike the relation of ownership (svasvāmibhāva). Moreover, as Jason Schwartz has recently demonstrated, in the early medieval Deccan, esoteric Śaiva institutions not only owned their land in perpetuity but held incontrovertible legal jurisdiction over the affairs conducted within those domains. Such jurisdiction afforded religious institutions the right to dictate what precisely constituted law within their domains, according to their religious precepts. 115
